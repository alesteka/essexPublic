Unit 1

In the first week we examined the importance of understanding risk management as this is an important process that empowers business with necessary tools identifying and dealing with potential risks. Some of the common steps include:

  • Identifying existing risks: The first step involves brainstorming. At this step, various teams come together to review sources of risks and create priority list of the risks that have to be dealt with more urgently.
  • Assessing the risks: Figuring out how to handle the risks, by scrutinizing the causes and possible influences on the business.
  • Developing appropriate response: Examining and implementing measures that can be taken to mitigate and avoid negative outcomes.
  • Developing preventive mechanisms: If the risks appear again, we can apply previous remediation techniques to face similar business risks.

Similarly, many other authors point out the importance of the PDCA Mandate, which is an iterative problem-solving technique consisting of four steps to identify and improve business operations:

  • Plan: Provides framework for operations, ideally reflecting company's mission and values.
  • Do: Develop, implement, and test the solution. Before work is carried out, standardization of roles, responsibilities, and methods must also be established.
  • Check: Gathering feedback from the teams and reviewing it. This ensures the project objective's are met with the potential future adjustments.
  • Act: Taking corrective actions on past mistakes.

Additionally, we examined qualitative and quantitative risk analysis approaches to help address risks and hazards faced by an organization:

  • Qualitative approach: Qualitative risk analysis is the process of evaluating and rating an identified risk based on a person’s perception or judgment.
  • Quantitative approach: Quantitative risk analysis is the process of calculating risk based on data gathered. The goal of quantitative risk analysis is to further specify how much will the impact of a risk cost the business. One of the go-to standard for the latter approach is Open FAIR, which helps to understand, analyse, and quantify information risk in financial terms. Furthermore, it provides compatibility with other well known standards, for example: ISO, COSO ERM, SABSA, COBIT, NIST, and OCTAVE. The latter, additionally, consists of three phases which include building enterprise-wide security requirements, identifying infrastructural vulnerabilities, and determining security risk management strategy.

(Lecturecasts, 2024), (CFI Team, n.d.), (Cuofano, 2024), (Hargrave & James, 2022), (SafetyCulture, 2024)

Additionally, we evaluated some of the publicly available threat modelling tools:

  • OWASP Threat Dragon: Is an open source threat modelling tool that is used to produce threat model diagrams as part of the safe development lifecycle.
  • Microsoft Threat modelling Tool: Open source software that aids in the detection of threats during the design phase of software projects.
  • ThreatModeler: Allows to safely build applications/infrastructure. It supports known regulatory standards like NIST, GDPR, and PCI.
  • IriusRisk: Is Security Development Lifecycle risk management platform that connects Security, Operations, and Development. It's built for integration, simplicity, scale, and speed. furthermore, it employs pre-defined components and a built-in threat and countermeasure library to enable teams to construct these models fast without relying on security professionals.

Unit 7

    In unit 7 we discussed Operating Systems (OS) and their relationships to programming languages and security. Most applications need to interact with the underlying operating system – whether it is to pick up input from a keyboard (or other input device) or to output data to a display. There are also security implications because such interactions often require the user application to link with software libraries – either supplied by the OS provider or a third party.
